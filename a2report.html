Subtheme: Exclusionary Zoning

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. Which municipalities most prominently have single-family zoning laws? In particular, are single-family zoning laws in Boston concentrated amongst certain municipalities, or are they distributed relatively evenly across all municipalities, especially in consideration of the size of the affected population within a municipality? Is there a geographic pattern to the distribution? I'm curious to investigate this because of my own personal experience in Washington state, where there is complexity to zoning laws not only within Seattle proper, but also (and especially) amongst cities on the eastside (e.g., Bellevue, Redmond, Renton, Tacoma, etc.). In these cities one can more prominently see a wealth gap and lack of housing affordability even more apparently. I'd be interested to know what the distribution looks like for municipalities in Boston.
  2. Are zoning laws tied to any specific urban planning features, such as public transportation locations, major highways, waterways, schools, industrial regions, etc? If so, are different types of urban planning features consistently considered more "desirable" to single-family zoning in Boston? I'm interested in this after reading the provided New York Times article (Cities Start to Question an American Ideal), which highlighted key urban planning features such as a new light rail (in LA), an industrial area (in North Carolina), and a major state route (in Georgia) as part of annotations in their visualizations.
  3. What is the racial demographic of different municipalities, and are there patterns across municipalities with more restrictive zoning laws? Does the data indeed seem to indicate that single-family zoning laws do indeed disproportionately affect minorities, such as people of color or low-income communities? I'd like to investigate this after reading and watching "Exclusionary by Design", where they discussed how intertwined zoning laws have been to the problem of affordable housing over the past decade in Boston. I'd like to look at race specifically considering that I've seen Boston and Massachusetts as a whole additionally have a reputation of being a predominantly white city from my friends outside of Massachusetts, and I'd imagine that that might impact the how the data compares between different municipalities.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

These visualizations thus paint an interesting picture of the data, although my primary reactions to the investigations have been many further questions and concerns about the data. I think that although the metadata provides very necessary information about what the data fields represent, I'd need to do a lot more investigation into how different numbers were aggregated and calculated from the various Census sources in order to feel confident about the different numbers provided in the data. Despite this, however, I think that my investigations demonstrated to me that there do appear that zoning patterns across Massachusetts municipalities are deeply uneven depending on the specific municipality. Indeed, it appears that race and especially income bracket can be very good estimators and indicators of what zoning restrictions in a certain Massachusetts municipality might look like, and during future investigations, I'd love to dive deeper into specific municipalities and look at zone-level data, trying to better identify particular zones of interest and patterns across them in order to have less aggregated, more differentiable insights into minority groups and low-income communities.
